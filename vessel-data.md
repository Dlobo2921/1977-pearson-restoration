## Design & Construction

Bill Shaw designed the 323 with a sharp entry, long waterline, sweet sheer, and slightly rounded vertical transom. A distinctive wide black stripe — the "raccoon" stripe — encircled the cabin trunk on production boats, offset by black-anodized spars.

**Hull:** Solid hand-laid fiberglass and polyester resin. No balsa in the hull sides.

**Deck:** Sandwich construction — end-grain balsa core between two fiberglass skins. Lighter and stiffer than solid glass. Prone to delamination where hardware was drilled without proper bedding; inspect all deck hardware annually.

**Hull-to-deck joint:** Chemically bonded and mechanically fastened with stainless self-tapping screws, also securing the teak toerail. Taped on the inside with fiberglass and resin.

**Ballast:** 4,500 lbs of lead set inside the keel, which is an integral part of the hull molding, then glassed over. No keel bolts to inspect or replace — the keel is the hull.

**Rudder:** Skeg-mounted. The skeg protects the rudder from grounding damage.

## What It Feels Like to Sail

She'll heel quickly to 17 degrees in moderate air. **Reefing threshold: 15 knots** — early by sport-boat standards, but correct for this design. A reefed 323 sailing in 18 knots is faster, more comfortable, and better controlled than an over-canvassed one.

Downwind: boom vang on, traveler eased, main not twisting. Unbalanced downwind setup causes rolling. Keep gear stowed forward of the mast when possible.

## Lake Michigan Notes

- **Draft:** 4.50 ft — can access harbors that deeper boats can't
- **Seiche buffer:** Reasonable margin in Lake Michigan seiche swings (1–2 ft water level shifts in hours); check tide board at harbor office during spring and after storms
- **Reefing threshold:** First reef before sustained 15 knots. First reef: main + partially furled jib. Second reef: 25+ knots, working jib.
- **Squall readiness:** Lake Michigan squalls can stack to 35 knots in under 20 minutes. Reef before the squall line arrives. Watch the western horizon.
- **Bridge clearance:** 45 ft 4 in — clears all Chicago-area fixed bridges. Confirm DuSable Bridge opening schedule for any Lake Shore Drive crossing.
- **Reverse walk:** Walks to port in reverse. Come in slightly to starboard of centerline; apply reverse and the stern tracks back toward center.

## Quirks Worth Knowing

- **Reverse walks to port:** Brief crew before any backing maneuver.
- **Companionway hatch:** May have a slight bind at ~2/3 open. Push slightly down and forward simultaneously.
- **Genoa sheet leads:** Track position matters. 130% genoa: front of track. Working jib: middle. Wrong position shows as flutter in the leech or bag in the foot.
- **Deck hardware:** Balsa-core deck — any hardware drilled without proper bedding can have a delamination pocket. Soft spots = wet balsa. Get them repaired before they grow.
- **Bilge:** Some water in the bilge is normal on a 1977 boat. Check before departure; pump if needed. Rapid accumulation = stop and find the source.
- **Chainplates:** Stainless steel chainplates are a known inspection item on 323s of this age. Crevice corrosion is invisible until it isn't. Pull and inspect every few years.
